# Lanthir Haven

Lanthir Haven — "Haven of Song-Light" — is a coastal settlement on the western shore of [[Aerinórë]], the sanctuary continent of the north. It is not a city in any mainland sense, but a living community of approximately 800 souls nestled where ancient old-growth forest meets white sand beach. The settlement grew organically over centuries as the natural meeting point between the continent's four peoples: [[Waldkyn]] of all eight variants (35%), Hollowkin — Halflings and Gnomes (25%), [[Vorok-Varr]] Orkin (20%), and [[Sael'anir]] Elves (15%), with a small number of occasional Mirennian visitors who found the place and could not bring themselves to leave.

The settlement has no formal government. Decisions emerge through consensus among the four peoples' elders, with disputes resolved in the Listening Circle — a natural beach amphitheater where every seat is equal and anyone may speak. The closest thing to a leader is Song-Mother Aelindra, an ancient Galapa Waldkyn who maintains the Song-Wards and is the only person all four communities defer to.

At the heart of Lanthir Haven stands the Great Singing Tree — a 600-foot coastal redwood whose root system extends a quarter-mile in every direction, threading through every building's foundation. Embedded thirty feet up the trunk, nestled in a natural hollow where root meets heartwood, is a fragment of immense power: the Gem, a piece of the [[Crown of Kael'Zorai]]. The tree hums constantly — a deep, subsonic vibration that IS the Song-Ward network protecting the entire continent. When the wind blows through its canopy, the humming resolves into something almost like words. The Gem's field of silence, balanced by the constant harmonic singing of the Waldkyn, has sustained the wards for fourteen centuries.

Lanthir Haven operates on a gift economy. No currency exists. The Hollowkin grow food, the Vorok-Varr tend livestock and build structures, the Waldkyn maintain the Song-Wards, and the Sael'anir navigate and defend the coastline. Everyone contributes. Nobody counts. Violence is unthinkable — Aerinórë has not seen a weapon drawn in anger for over 400 years. It is, by every measure, a paradise — and that is what makes its vulnerability so devastating.
